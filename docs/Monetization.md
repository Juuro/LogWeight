# Monetization Strategy

This document records a responsible monetization plan for LogWeight.

## Product constraints

LogWeight is intentionally small:

- one job: get body weight into Apple Health quickly.
- no ads, no analytics, no social mechanics, no dark patterns.
- no separate weight database and no lock-in.
- the entry flow must stay calm and distraction-free.

Any monetization must respect those constraints.

## Recommendation

Ship LogWeight as a **free download** and monetize with an **optional one-time supporter purchase**.

Recommended pricing:

- `Support LogWeight` — **$2.99**
- optional higher-value support tiers, if needed later: **$5.99** and **$9.99**

Use this as a voluntary contribution, not as a gate around the app's core purpose.

## What stays free

The following should remain available without payment:

- saving weight to Apple Health
- viewing recent history
- using Apple Watch / iPhone / iPad / Mac entry surfaces
- unit preferences and basic settings

These are the core promise of the app. Charging for them would make the product feel hostile for a utility that exists to remove friction.

## What supporters get

The cleanest first version is that supporters mainly get the satisfaction of funding development, plus a small acknowledgement such as:

- a discreet `Supporter` badge in Settings
- optional alternate app icons or cosmetic thank-you extras
- early access to clearly non-essential quality-of-life additions

Do **not** put faster logging, Apple Health access, or history behind a paywall.

## Placement and UX rules

- Keep purchase prompts out of the main entry flow.
- Put the supporter call-to-action in **Settings** only.
- Show it calmly and infrequently; no full-screen paywalls.
- Never block Save behind a purchase sheet.
- Never use countdowns, streak pressure, or guilt-based copy.

## Why this model fits LogWeight

1. **Low risk for users.** People can verify the app solves their problem before paying.
2. **Fits the app's size.** A subscription would feel excessive for a single-purpose utility.
3. **Protects trust.** No ads and no tracking preserves the privacy-first positioning.
4. **Keeps the product honest.** Revenue depends on goodwill, so the app stays simple and useful.

## Fallback option

If optional in-app support proves too weak, the next-best model is a **low one-time upfront price** around **$1.99-$2.99**.

Even in that case:

- avoid subscriptions
- avoid ads
- keep pricing simple
- keep feature access identical across paying users

## Review checkpoint

Revisit this strategy after launch if one of these becomes true:

- support revenue does not cover ongoing App Store costs
- users repeatedly ask for a direct way to support the app
- the app grows beyond a single focused utility

Until then, the default stance should be: **free core utility, optional low-cost support, zero pressure**.
